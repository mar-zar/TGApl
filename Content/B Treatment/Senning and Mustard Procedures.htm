Senning and Mustard Procedures

The Mustard and Senning procedures are two similar operations for TGA. Each is named for the surgeon who first performed the operation. In both, the surgeon creates a two-way baffle in the top part of the heart, which serves as a bridge between the two sides of the heart. The difference in this procedures is that in a Senning procedure, the surgeon uses the patient’s own tissue to create the baffle while in the Mustard procedure, a synthetic material is used.

These procedures are called “atrial switch procedures” because there is a baffle through the heart’s top part, or atria, which allows the blood to reach the ventricles.

The Senning procedure was invented by Swedish cardiac surgeon Åke Senning, who is also known for implanting the first permanent cardiac pacemaker in 1958 and performing (together with Clarence Crafoord), the first successful open-heart surgery on humans in Europe using the heart-lung machine in 1953.

The Mustard procedure was developed in 1963 by Dr. William Mustard at the Hospital for Sick Children in Toronto, Canada as an alternative and simplified technique to the Senning procedure.

From that time both Senning and Mustard procedures became the standard operation for d-TGA in different parts of the world saving lives of thousands of children. Long-term follow-up studies now extend to more than 40 years post-operation and there are numerous patients thriving in their 50s.

Both Senning and Mustard procedures were largely replaced in the late 1980s by the Jatene procedure (arterial switch operation).
